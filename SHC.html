Specific Heat Capacity

The specific heat capacity of a substance is the amount of energy required to change the temperature of one kilogram of the substance by one degree Celsius.

The amount of energy stored in or released from a system as its temperature changes can be calculated using the equation:

change in thermal energy = mass x specific heat capacity x temperature change

ΔE = mcΔθ

select and apply

change in thermal energy, ΔE, in joules, J

mass, m, in kilograms, kg

specific heat capacity, c, in joules per kilogram per degree Celsius, J/kg°C

temperature change, Δθ, in degrees Celsius, °C

Δθ = Final Temperature - Initial Temperature

Rearrange to find:

mass - m = ΔE/cΔθ

specific heat capacity - c = ΔE/mΔθ

temperature change - Δθ = ΔE/mc

Worked example

Calculate the temperature change when 84 000 J of energy is supplied to 500 g of water.

Specific Heat Capacity of water is 4200 J/kg°C

ΔE = mcΔθ

84000 = 0.5 x 4200 x Δθ

Δθ = 84000 / 0.5 x 4200

Δθ = 40°C
